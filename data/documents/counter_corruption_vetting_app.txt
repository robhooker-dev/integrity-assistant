Based on College of Policing APP | Updated to reflect Vetting APP 2024

PART 1: COUNTER CORRUPTION

1.1 DEFINITION OF POLICE CORRUPTION

Police corruption is defined as a member of a law enforcement agency or related organisation abusing their position for personal gain, or the gain of another, and corruptly or dishonestly exercising or failing to exercise their powers or duties.

1.2 NATIONAL CORRUPTION CATEGORIES

The NPCC, in consultation with the College of Policing, defines the following categories of corrupt conduct:

Category 1 — Perverting the course of justice
Conduct that undermines or interferes with the fair and proper administration of justice, including fabricating evidence, making false statements, or interfering with witnesses.

Category 2 — Misuse of force systems and information
Unauthorised access to, disclosure of, or interference with police systems, intelligence, or information. Includes sharing police information with criminals or third parties without authority.

Category 3 — Abuse of position for sexual purpose
Using a policing position to engage in, or attempt to engage in, sexual activity or a relationship with a member of the public encountered through policing. This is a serious form of corruption and is treated as gross misconduct in all cases.

Category 4 — Corrupt relationships
Maintaining relationships with criminals or others who seek to compromise policing, including undeclared notifiable associations, or providing cover or protection to criminal activity.

Category 5 — Bribery, fraud and theft
Accepting bribes, committing fraud, or theft in connection with policing duties. Includes inappropriate acceptance of gifts or hospitality.

Category 6 — Misuse of substances
Drug use or misuse of controlled substances by officers or staff, including use of steroids or other performance-enhancing drugs not prescribed for medical purposes.

1.3 THE NATIONAL THREAT ASSESSMENT

Forces are required to maintain a counter-corruption threat assessment and control strategy, reviewed annually, identifying the corruption threats specific to the force area.

1.4 PROACTIVE COUNTER-CORRUPTION CAPABILITY

Forces must maintain both overt and covert counter-corruption capability. The Counter Corruption Unit (CCU) or Anti-Corruption Unit (ACU) is responsible for:
- Receiving and assessing corruption intelligence
- Conducting overt and covert investigations into corrupt conduct
- Managing notifiable associations
- Delivering corruption awareness training
- Supporting vetting functions

1.5 CORRUPTION INTELLIGENCE

All officers and staff have a duty to report suspected corrupt conduct or corruption intelligence. The duty to report applies regardless of the rank or grade of the subject.

Reports can be made:
- To the CCU/ACU directly
- To a line manager (who must then refer to CCU/ACU)
- Via the force confidential reporting system
- To the IOPC in appropriate cases

The obligation to report is not affected by personal loyalty or friendship. The failure to report known corruption is itself a breach of the Standards of Professional Behaviour.

1.6 CODE OF ETHICS — INTEGRITY PRINCIPLE

The College of Policing Code of Ethics (2024) sets out the following in relation to integrity:
- You act in the honest and ethical manner. You do not compromise or abuse your position.
- You are open and transparent about your actions and decisions.
- You report, challenge or take action against the dishonest or unethical behaviour of others.
- You do not allow your personal interests to conflict with your position or your duty.

PART 2: VETTING

2.1 VETTING CODE OF PRACTICE 2023

The Vetting Code of Practice (2023) establishes the mandatory framework for police vetting in England and Wales. All forces must comply with the Code and the supporting APP for Vetting (2024).

2.2 LEVELS OF VETTING

Management Vetting (MV) — The minimum level for all officers and staff upon appointment.

Enhanced Management Vetting (EMV) — Required for roles involving access to sensitive intelligence, covert policing, or counter-corruption functions.

Developed Vetting (DV) — Required for roles involving access to the most sensitive national security material. Conducted by the National Security Vetting Service.

Special Procedure Vetting (SPV) — Applied in specific circumstances where an enhanced assessment of an individual's suitability is required.

2.3 CONTINUOUS VETTING

Vetting is not a one-time process. The 2024 APP introduced:
- Mandatory periodic re-vetting throughout an officer's career
- Mandatory randomised re-vetting
- Requirement for immediate vetting review when adverse information is received, regardless of length of service

2.4 ADVERSE INFORMATION

Officers and staff must disclose any adverse changes in their personal circumstances that may affect their vetting clearance. This includes:

(a) Criminal investigations, charges, cautions or convictions
(b) Financial difficulties, bankruptcy or CCJs
(c) Notifiable associations (see Notifiable Associations Policy)
(d) Changes in household composition involving persons with criminal records
(e) Travel to, or contact with, persons from countries subject to security concern
(f) Drug use

2.5 WITHDRAWAL OF VETTING

Vetting clearance may be withdrawn or downgraded where adverse information is identified. Withdrawal of vetting may result in:
- Transfer to a non-sensitive role
- Restricted duties
- In serious cases, proceedings for dismissal

2.6 VETTING AND MISCONDUCT

A failure to disclose relevant information at vetting, or a failure to update the force when circumstances change, is a breach of the Standards of Professional Behaviour and may constitute misconduct or gross misconduct.

2.7 CASEY REVIEW RECOMMENDATIONS

The Casey Review (2023) identified significant failures in police vetting, including:
- Failure to vet or re-vet appropriately
- Inconsistent application of national standards
- Failure to act on adverse information already held by forces

The 2024 APP was developed in response to these recommendations to strengthen and standardise the vetting process across all forces.

PART 3: PROFESSIONAL STANDARDS

3.1 STANDARDS OF PROFESSIONAL BEHAVIOUR

The Standards of Professional Behaviour apply to all police officers. Equivalent standards apply to police staff. The Standards cover:

1. Honesty and Integrity
2. Authority, Respect and Courtesy
3. Equality and Diversity
4. Use of Force
5. Orders and Instructions
6. Duties and Responsibilities
7. Confidentiality
8. Fitness for Duty
9. Discreditable Conduct
10. Challenging and Reporting Improper Conduct

3.2 MISCONDUCT AND GROSS MISCONDUCT

Misconduct is a breach of the Standards of Professional Behaviour.
Gross misconduct is a breach so serious that it would justify dismissal.

3.3 REPORTING OBLIGATIONS

Standard 10 — Challenging and Reporting Improper Conduct — requires officers to:
- Report any conduct which they believe constitutes misconduct or gross misconduct
- Challenge improper conduct by fellow officers or staff
- Not ignore or facilitate the improper conduct of others

This standard creates a positive duty to report, not merely a permission to do so.
